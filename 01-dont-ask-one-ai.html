#1: Don't Ask One AI — Ask Three

You've got a new task in front of you — maybe a complex feature to analyze, a strategy to develop, or a problem you're not sure how to approach. The "Council of LLMs" approach helps. Not by replacing your thinking, but by giving you a room full of sparring partners who never get tired, never judge you, and each see things a little differently.

Why Different Models Think Differently

Every LLM was trained on different data, with different objectives, and different fine-tuning. ChatGPT tends to be thorough and tool-oriented. Claude tends to be crisp, structured, and good at reasoning. Gemini is well-connected to Google's knowledge graph and useful for research. These aren't marketing claims. You'll actually see different outputs when you run the same prompt through each.

The point isn't to pick a winner. It's to collect perspectives. One model might surface a risk you hadn't considered. Another might challenge an assumption the first one made. When you synthesize their outputs yourself, the result is often better than any single response.

  • ChatGPT / Codex Company — Well-rounded, strong with tool use, good at generating diverse ideas. Access individually approved — check with your team lead if you don't have it yet.

  • Google Gemini (Workspace) Free — AI integrated into Google Docs, Sheets, Gmail. Different from AI Studio — this is about bringing AI into documents you already use. Strength: seamless in your workflow. Weakness: less powerful for standalone analysis.
  • Perplexity Free — perplexity.ai. AI-powered search with cited sources. Strength: best for research — every answer comes with links to sources. Weakness: not great for creative or analytical tasks.
  • Mistral Free — chat.mistral.ai. French AI lab, strong open-source models. Strength: fast, good at code, privacy-focused (EU-based). Weakness: smaller knowledge base than GPT/Claude, less polished for conversations.

You don't need to use all of them every time. But for decisions that matter — like designing a strategy for a new feature — running the same prompt through two or three of them and comparing the outputs is genuinely useful.

One LLM, Many Interfaces

When you hear "use multiple AI tools", the instinct is to think about switching between ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini. That is a good start. But there is a layer most people miss: even within a single LLM, the interface you choose shapes what you get.

Take Claude as an example. Four interfaces, four different experiences — and they differ not just in features, but also in performance and capabilities:

  • Claude Web with Connectors: gives the model access to your email, Google Drive, and other services. Want a summary of your unread inbox? This is where you go.
  • Claude Desktop App: same as web, but with direct access to local files and folders on your computer — no uploading needed. Can also be faster and more responsive than the browser version. Available for Windows and macOS.
  • Claude Code (CLI and IDE integration): built for technical work. File operations, codebase questions, structured reasoning about code. It behaves more like an engineer and less like a chat assistant.
  • Chrome Extension: works directly on whatever page you have open. Useful for quick analysis of what is in front of you, without switching context.

Same underlying model, four meaningfully different experiences. The interface shapes the context, the defaults, the performance, and ultimately what the model focuses on. The Desktop App, for example, can access your local filesystem directly — something the web version can't do without uploading files first.

So your council does not have to be three different LLMs. It can also be three different modes of the same one. A productivity question might go to Claude Web with Connectors. A code review stays in Claude Code. A quick read of a page you are looking at goes to the extension. Picking the right interface for the right task is a skill in itself. Match the tool to the job.

How to Run a Council

The workflow is simpler than it sounds. Start by writing a clear, detailed prompt. Something like this:

Act as an expert in [your domain].
I need to [describe task].

The constraints are:
- [constraint 1]
- [constraint 2]
- [constraint 3]

Also flag the things most people forget,
and highlight the biggest risks.

Now run this in ChatGPT, Claude, and optionally Gemini. Don't tweak the prompt between runs. You want comparability. Then read all three outputs and look for:

  • Ideas that appear in all three: these are probably important
  • Ideas that only appear in one: these might be the most valuable insights
  • Contradictions: these are worth investigating further

You're the curator. The AI generates, you judge. That's the division of labor that works.

If you want to run a council without switching between browser tabs, LM Arena (lmarena.ai) lets you compare any two LLMs side by side with the same prompt. It's free and cuts down the friction of running comparisons.

Roles You Can Assign

You can make the council even more useful by assigning different roles to different models (or different conversations with the same model):

  • Generator: brainstorm as many ideas as possible, no filtering
Act as a creative brainstormer.
Generate as many ideas as possible for [your task].
Don't filter or judge — quantity over quality.
I'll curate afterwards.
  • Critic: find gaps and weaknesses in a plan you already have
Act as a critical reviewer.
Here is my current approach: [paste your approach].
Find the weaknesses, blind spots, and things I'm missing.
Be honest — I want to improve this, not hear that it's great.
  • Devil's Advocate: argue why the current approach is wrong
Act as a devil's advocate.
I'm planning to [describe your plan].
Argue against it. Tell me why this will fail.
What am I not seeing?
  • Researcher: look up known issues with similar situations (enable web search)
Act as a researcher with web access.
I'm working on [describe topic].
Find known issues, common pitfalls, and lessons learned
from similar situations. Cite your sources.

Assigning roles isn't just a neat trick. It changes the model's behavior meaningfully. Ask Claude to be a critic and it will find things it wouldn't surface if you just asked for ideas.

💡 One important note on data privacy: don't paste real customer data, internal code, or anything confidential into public AI tools. Anonymize or use synthetic examples. When in doubt, ask.

Your Exercise

Pick a task you're currently working on — something medium-complexity rather than trivial. Then do both parts:

Part 1: Compare Different LLMs

Write one clear prompt describing what you need. Then run the exact same prompt (don't change a word) in both ChatGPT and Claude. Compare the outputs side by side:

  • What does one surface that the other doesn't?
  • Where do they agree? Where do they contradict each other?
  • Which output would you actually use — and why?

Part 2: Same LLM, Different Instructions

Now pick one of the two LLMs and run your task through it twice — but with different roles:

  • First run: Use your original prompt as-is.
  • Second run: Add a role — try the "Critic" prompt from above: "Act as a critical reviewer and find the weaknesses in this approach: [paste your current approach]."

Compare the two outputs. Notice how the same model gives you meaningfully different results just by changing the framing.

Both parts together take about 20 minutes. You'll likely come out with ideas or risks you hadn't considered. That's the whole point: different perspectives — whether from different models or different instructions — make your thinking sharper.
